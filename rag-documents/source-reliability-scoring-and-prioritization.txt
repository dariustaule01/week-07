Source Reliability, Indicator Confidence, and Prioritization Rules

The Feed Collector should not assume that every public threat intelligence source has the same reliability. The AI-Capstone-Threat-Intel project needs a way to preserve evidence while also helping downstream components decide which items deserve attention first. Source reliability and indicator confidence are related but different. Source reliability describes the general trust level of the publisher or feed. Indicator confidence describes how strongly the collected evidence supports a specific conclusion.

High-reliability sources usually include official vendor advisories, government cybersecurity agencies, major vulnerability databases, well-known security research organizations, and primary reports from organizations that directly observed the activity. Medium-reliability sources may include reputable blogs, community-maintained feeds, and secondary summaries that cite primary sources. Low-reliability sources may include unverified social posts, copied indicator dumps, vague summaries without sources, or feeds that frequently include stale or incorrect indicators. A source can still be useful even when it is low reliability, but the system should not treat it as final proof.

Indicator confidence should be based on evidence. An IP address or domain should have higher confidence if the source explains why it is malicious, provides observation dates, links it to malware behavior, or is confirmed by multiple independent sources. Confidence should be lower when the indicator appears without context, is old, is associated with shared hosting, or is only mentioned in a copied list. The collector should preserve the original sentence or paragraph around the indicator when possible because context is often what determines whether the indicator is meaningful.

Prioritization should consider severity, exploitability, relevance, recency, and confidence. A recently published vulnerability affecting widely used software should rank higher than an old low-context indicator. A threat report that includes active exploitation, ransomware deployment, credential theft, or confirmed compromise should rank higher than a general awareness article. Recency matters because threat intelligence loses value over time, especially for infrastructure indicators such as IP addresses and URLs. However, older reports can still be useful for understanding tactics, techniques, procedures, and long-running actor behavior.

The Feed Collector should provide downstream scoring features rather than making final decisions alone. Useful fields include source_reliability, confidence_hint, first_seen, last_seen, published_at, related_cves, affected_products, indicator_types, source_count, and evidence_snippet. A downstream prioritization component can combine these fields with business context. For example, a vulnerability in software used by the organization should be more important than a vulnerability in an irrelevant product.

The system should also avoid overcounting repeated information. If ten feeds copy the same original advisory, that should not count as ten independent confirmations. The collector should track canonical sources and related sources. Independent confirmation means that separate sources appear to have separate evidence, not merely duplicate content. A good record can include both the original source and any secondary sources that repeated it.

For the capstone, a practical scoring scheme can use simple categories: source reliability of high, medium, or low; indicator confidence of high, medium, low, or unknown; and priority of urgent, elevated, normal, or monitor. Urgent items should involve active exploitation, critical severity, high-confidence evidence, or direct relevance to protected assets. Elevated items are important but may need more review. Normal items are useful for background awareness. Monitor items should be retained but not treated as immediate action items.

The goal of scoring is not to replace analyst judgment. The goal is to organize large amounts of collected information so that a human or later AI component can focus on the most important evidence first. The Feed Collector supports this by preserving source context, capturing reliability signals, and linking related records without hiding uncertainty.
